The Math Square

An affordable online subscription platform offering mathematics notes and video lessons in Afrikaans. Designed for high school learners, it makes complex concepts simple, accessible and engaging — helping students build confidence, improve marks, and learn at their own pace in their home language.

Problem

Many Afrikaans-speaking students struggle to find affordable, easy-to-understand mathematics resources in their home language. Existing resources are often expensive, poorly structured, or entirely English-based, leaving a large gap in support.

Solution

A mobile-friendly, easy-to-navigate platform offering structured mathematics notes and video lessons, entirely in Afrikaans, focused on building concept clarity and confidence.

Key Features

  • Subscription-based pricing — paid and free versions.
  • Consumable content — short video explanations and detailed notes.
  • Accessible & feedback-driven — mobile-friendly design with continuous knowledge testing and feedback mechanisms.

Playing to Win - Making Choices

Finding a strategic framework is not easy...believe me, as an exco member, I've gone through multiple iterations of strategy with the hope of "this is the one" every time a new consultant walks through the door.

Over the past 7 years in start-up environments, working very closely with founders, the one approach that has made the most sense to me (a very practical and competitive person) was the playing to win framework.

▾ Read full case study

How we got here

This framework was shared with me by one of my mentors in 2025, he is a no BS straightshooter with loads of experience in the product space, and it all started with a single question in one of our afternoon discussions: "Are you winning?", I wanted to respond, but before I could he said, "Where are you winning, not almost, not partially, not maybe, where are you without a doubt the winner" and the answer was simple, nowhere.

I came to the obvious realisation, that to be the best (win), we first had to be the best at something, one thing, a simple thing and that comes with making hard choices.

Making choices

What struck me immediately was how uncomfortable it made me...but in a good way. The framework doesn't allow for any vague ambitions, middle-ground thinking, or "we'll figure it out later." It forced me to answer five deceptively simple questions:

  • What does winning mean?
  • Where will we play?
  • How will we win?
  • What capabilities do we need?
  • What systems support this?

As a Head of Product, I constantly made decisions. What do we build next? Who are we really building for? Which problems deserve attention now, and which fires can burn? Before Playing to Win, I often evaluated decisions in isolation, feature by feature, initiative by initiative, customer by customer, or even keeping a few doors open for "just in case".

This new way of thinking pushed me to zoom out and ask: Does this choice reinforce how we've decided to win, or does it dilute it?

And that is the question I lead with. Will this help us win.

The impact

I became far more comfortable saying no. I always enjoyed to push back and get to the real "why", now I had an additional tool to evaluate if the fit in. I stopped trying to optimise for every user, every segment, and every possible outcome. Instead, decisions became easier because they were anchored to a few explicit strategic bets.

It also made me more honest with myself as a leader. Playing to Win exposes gaps quickly (The scary part). If you can't clearly articulate how you win, it's often because the strategy isn't clear and not because it is "complex" as I used to fool myself. That realisation helped me slow down when needed, do the hard thinking, and avoid premature execution.

Perhaps the biggest personal takeaway is this: strategy is not a document or a workshop lead by a expensive consultant, it is purely a discipline.

Playing to Win gave me a mental model I now use daily. It sits behind roadmap decisions, prioritisation calls, hiring choices, and even how I frame product narratives internally (Made a huge impact on conveying value and getting buy-in).

Final thoughts

I still use other tools like OKRs, I still track metrics and I still love doing experiments but Playing to Win is the lens that keeps theses things (and me) aligned. It reminds me that my job isn't to maximise activity, but to make choices that increase the chance of winning.

Taking Control of Adoption: Product Fruits

TL;DR
  • Shipping features isn't enough…users need guidance to unlock value.
  • Building in-app guidance with engineering support is slow, rigid, and not scalable.
  • Initially, we made the mistake of hardcoding onboarding tools.
  • After learning from the community, we implemented Product Fruits, a no-code solution that empowered the product team directly.
  • The results: 85% user engagement with guides, 132% increase in feature awareness, and a 12-day reduction in time-to-first-value.
▾ Read full case study

As Product People, we often get stuck in a loop. Features get shipped, but adoption lags. Most of the time the problem isn't product quality — it's the lack of user education and guided onboarding, especially in complex or ever-changing platforms.

At the same time, engineering resources are limited, and every minor change to help users understand the product often ends up at the bottom of the backlog. Developing platform guidance features typically takes significant time and lacks flexibility, which makes fast iteration nearly impossible, and that is what you need when it comes to driving adoption.

As a result, product teams are often at the mercy of Customer Success or Marketing to relay critical product messages and feature changes to users manually, which is inefficient and inconsistent.

Challenges we faced as Product People
  • Product updates get released, but users aren't fully aware of what changed or how to use new features.
  • Limited engineering capacity made it difficult to implement tooltips, product tours, or onboarding nudges quickly.
  • Manual walkthroughs from Customer Success or Marketing don't scale, and users still drop off before reaching value.

The journey I went on

I knew we were delivering value to users, all the user engagement, research and gut feels indicated it, but we lacked quick adoption. So the journey to improve user adoption started, one that didn't begin perfectly.

Initially, I made the mistake of having parts of our feedback loop and feature education custom-built into the product (I was desperate). This meant pulling in valuable engineering time to create hardcoded tooltips, banners, and help modals, all of which were difficult to maintain and quickly iterate on.

It wasn't until I started openly talking about this challenge with other product people in my network that I realized we were solving a common problem in a very resource-heavy way. Through these conversations, I discovered the no-code enablement category and began exploring tools that could let our team move faster and be more flexible.

After a bit of research, I (with the help of our CTO) quickly implemented Product Fruits, a no-code user guidance platform that gave our product team the ability to create and launch product tours, checklists, tooltips, and announcements without requiring engineering resources; design role-based onboarding experiences that adapt to each user's specific needs; and deploy in-app NPS surveys and feedback widgets to gather real-time UX insights (though this last piece was only tested in QA, never pushed to production).

The Impact

While we could see the impact clearly in some key metrics, the qualitative feedback from users and our Customer Success team was overwhelmingly positive.

85%of users engaged with tours/guides
+132%new feature engagement vs. email-open
-80%recurring training sessions needed
-12 daystime-to-first-value for new users

What I Learned

  • Shipping is only half the job. Adoption needs intentional, persistent guidance.
  • No-code tools help bridge the gap between product and users without waiting for engineering.
  • Small nudges drive big results. Even a single tooltip can make a world of difference for a user.
  • Experimentation is the key to finding what works.

Product Strategy Framework - The Biddle-ish model

TL;DR

This Product Strategy framework, big brained by Gibson Biddle, has become one of my go-to tools for developing product strategies that align with business goals and delivering real value.

While every business is different, this framework provides a solid foundation for shaping high-level hypotheses, prioritizing work, and connecting product thinking to measurable outcomes.

It looks at a lot of acronyms so LFG:

  • Delight, Hard to Copy, Margin-Enhancing
  • Get Big, Lead, Expand
  • Growth, Engagement, Monetization
  • Strategy, Metric, Tactics
  • Roadmaps

I've applied it across projects, including JOBJACK and most recently The Math Square, and it's consistently helped me turn abstract ideas into concrete strategies.

▾ Read full case study

Why This Framework?

Most product frameworks are either too abstract or too tactical. This framework strikes the right balance for me by guiding product teams through a clear, holistic thinking process. Everything from product positioning all the way to tactics (or Bets as I call them) and metrics. It's designed to align product teams with leadership and customer needs, something every company, at any stage, can benefit from.

Whether I'm building strategy for a marketplace, SaaS product, or internal tooling, I start with this approach and adapt it to the context of the business or problem.

Get clarity

  • What is the product? (so simple a 5 year old needs to understand this)
  • How does it make lives better?
  • What is the product's personality?
My Thoughts

This exercise forces simplicity and acts as a litmus test for alignment. It's not just for marketing, it influences how we pitch to execs, how we prioritize features, and how we write product copy.

Every strategy I've led begins with asking the team: "What exactly are we building, and why does it matter?" The goal is to box the product into a single, simple idea.

DHM

How will your product Delight customers in Hard-to-copy, Margin-enhancing ways?

  • Delight = Ease, speed, joy, empowerment
  • Hard to Copy = Data flywheels, unique workflows, proprietary logic
  • Margin-Enhancing = Business model viability

These DHM strategies become the backbone of your product roadmap, it guides your decision making and ensure you keep your eyes on the prize.

My Thoughts

Reviewing initiatives or ideas, I need to often remind myself to forget about my opinions and thoughts and ask myself - Will this actually make the user smile, save them time or make them feel empowered? Being a delight, does not mean it is flashy or fancy, it needs to be meaningful.

Ensuring your product is hard to copy is not about features…Features are easy to copy…What is hard to copy is data loops, ecosystems, and intuitive product behavior based on user context. Product teams often forget about Margins, but it's critical. We're not just here to build, we're here to build a sustainable business.

GLE

You then go on and break long-term strategy into three parts to get direction.

  • What do you want to Get Big On → What core value or wedge are we doubling down on?
  • What aspect of the market do you want to Lead → Where can we become the category leader?
  • What do you want to Expand Into → What's our longer-term horizon?

This approach turns your vague vision statement into a phased bet with direction.

My Thoughts

What I have learned is that you CAN NOT DO THIS ON YOUR OWN. You need to ensure there is alignment from leadership as your vision for the product might differ from the CEO's which might differ from the CTO's.

With The Math Square:

  • Get Big On: Online high school mathematics recourses in South Africa.
  • Lead: Automated hiring based on algorithmic shortlisting.
  • Expand Into: Paid courses, CV services, and integrations with external ATS systems.

GEM

The GEM model prioritizes:

  • Growth → How do we acquire and expand our user base?
  • Engagement → How do we know the product is delivering value?
  • Monetization → Are we building a viable business?
My Thoughts

You can't prioritize everything. GEM helps us decide what matters most in the current season. For The Math Square we approach it as follows:

Growth: How do we acquire and expand our user base?

  • Primary Metric
    • New learner signups per month
    • WAU
  • Goal: Increase monthly signups from 30 to 100 in Q2 through organic channels and social media marketing

Engagement: How do we know the product is delivering value?

  • Primary Metric
    • % of users completing at least 1 lesson per week
  • Goal: Improve weekly activity completion from X% to Y% by increasing motivation through automated nudges. (We do not have insight into these metrics yet)

Monetization: How does The Math Square make money?

  • Primary Metric
    • MRR
  • Goal: Reach R10 000 in MRR by Q4

SMT

This is where it all connects:

  • Strategy: A DHM hypothesis
    • Example: Build trust by showing job seekers why they were unsuccessful
  • Metric: A measurable proxy tied to success
    • Example:
      • % of job seekers viewing rejection explanation
      • Reduction in support queries related to rejection reasons
  • Tactics: The actual projects that deliver it
    • Example: Add "Why I was unsuccessful?" to the job seeker's My Jobs section
My Thoughts

A product strategy without tactics and metrics is just a cool idea. This approach ensured we tested before scaling, and connected strategy directly to implementation.

Roadmaps That Tell a Story

I apply a rolling 4-quarter roadmap structured by strategy. Each row is a strategic anchor, and each column is a time-bound initiative. It's simple but powerful, it helps my teams visualize progress while ensuring we're working on the right things, not just shipping features.

Final thoughts…(Like Jerry used to say)

Strategy doesn't need to be complicated—but it does need to be clear. And a great strategy isn't just setting vision, it's connecting vision to work that delivers impact.

The Netflix Product Strategy Framework helps me do exactly that. Whether I'm building new features, refining an MVP, or driving long-term roadmap planning, it gives me a structure to think deeply, act purposefully, and measure effectively.
